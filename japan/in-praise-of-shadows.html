Personal Reflections

I’m beginning to think I have blinders on, or the equivalent of rose-colored glasses for a person who perceives the world traveling in a direction that will lead to disappointment and despair. My first read on my new intellectual journey — roughly, Japanese aesthetics — was In Praise of Shadows by Jun’ichirō Tanizaki. It seemed straightforwardly to be about that for the first ten pages or so. Lots of discussion about the particular type of paper the Japanese prefer for the walls of their homes to cast just the right light, the superiority of patina to polished metals, toilets, also. And as far as it went it’s an interesting conceit. The interplay between light and dark, illumination and shadow, is underappreciated. Tanizaki seems to think this delight in shadow may have arisen from some Japanese, or Asian more broadly, character traits that are not present in the West. That seems plausible to me. If shadow, and its correlate age, fits in better with a culture that largely still views itself (or did, at least, in the 1930s) as part of a long, unbroken chain of history, the modern West surely wants no part of it.

My great uncle, Stanley P. Wagner, wrote a book called The End of Revolution. I didn’t know he had done this until I was in my twenties, at which point I read it immediately. It’s an academic text devoid of a lot of jargon, and the thesis is that the acceleration of social and technological phenomena over the first half of the 20th century will eventually reach a terminal velocity, at which point massive change will no longer register with people. Previous generations will seem so distant, and our own pasts will be so ephemeral, that everything will be a kind of perpetual present. This also reminds me of Mark Fisher’s Capitalist Realism, which makes a similar argument about the commodification of the past and the way that halts culture in a perpetual present. So it seems the shadow of my own past, in the specter of my great uncle, looms over me, and maybe is partly to blame for this mood of mine that sees modern life as misguided, soulless, and unwittingly turning itself into fodder for the machine.

Tanizaki’s last few pages speak presciently to these fears. Not to get too woo, but these kinds of coincidences always make me wonder whether God is sending me messages through my favorite hobby. I found Tanizaki through a desire for respite from thinkers like David Bentley Hart, Iain McGilchrist, Paul Kingsnorth, and others who see real viciousness in our collective desire to turn ourselves into fuel for machines — The Machine — by casting off what makes us human. Instead, Tanizaki showed me another perspective on the same problem. Thankfully, it was subtle and interesting in the way the contrast between shadows of varying weights on a paper wall or alcove should be.
