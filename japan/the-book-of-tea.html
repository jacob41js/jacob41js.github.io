Personal Reflections

Okakura wants to draw a clear demarcation between what he sees as properly Japanese culture and western decadence. And I agree the points he emphasizes are beautiful, especially when it comes to setting up one’s tea room, the avoidance of perfect symmetry and repetition, the simplicity of it all. I do think it’s worth drawing a distinction, however, between the greatest thinkers in western thought — the ancients like Heraclitus, Plotinus, Epicurus, and even more recent ones like Maimonides and Thoreau — and the dominating forces of western imperialism and capitalism. Then again, perhaps there’s no distinction to be made. If a culture capitulates to one dominant mode, does it really matter what strange and contrary undercurrents it might contain?

In Japanese aesthetics what I’m understanding so far is that there is an emphasis on subtlety, simplicity, and nature. See Tanizaki’s description of the multifarious nature of shadow and how it sets a mood depending on the time of day, or this essay’s depiction of a single beautiful flower laid appropriately in a tea room. Haiku as a form of art in itself is a perfect encapsulation of these ideas. But at the same time there’s this striving for perfection that runs counter to the imperfection of nature. A perfect imperfection — it’s very zen koan. The idea is, somehow, the tea master can surmount this paradox, but it involves a certain giving up of oneself.

What does that mean — giving up of oneself? Although Okakura contrasts Taoism and Confucianism throughout the book, my sense is that the tea master must embody aspects of both. On the one hand a tea master must dutifully respect the tea ritual. There are prescriptions for bringing forth the best water, three types of boiling to be aware of, the color of the cups the tea is served in to best enhance the sensual experience of the tea, and the type and mode of conversation to match the setting. On the other you have the Taoist/Zennist current that seeks to emulate nature, that pays close attention to the subtleties of color and the placement of art, and the way that the environment plays with our internal experience. To be a successful tea master requires all of this. It reminds me a bit of e.e. cummings and how in order to be as free and iconoclastic with syntax in his poetry as he was, he had to study English grammar at Harvard for years. He couldn’t “break” the system without first being a master of it. I don’t know yet where this will come out in post-WWII Japanese culture. I’m sure it’s there. But what’s imported to the US nowadays seems diametrically opposed to these principles. Maybe the west won the cultural war and that’s that. Japan has just made those values its own in a certain way. That’s painting with too broad a brushstroke, though. Kawaii and Tokyo’s neon aura and anime are not the totality of Japan. I’m sure these ideas are still embedded in their art, however much western consumerism may try to sweep them away. In fact, Studio Ghibli films probably have a lot of these motifs in them.

Regardless of the critical lens, I like the idea of this aesthetic and would do well to try to incorporate it more into my life. I listened to a podcast with Chuck Klosterman recently where he was talking about how kids today can have true opinions about music from the ’60s and ’70s because they are not looking at it with a critical lens or with a superabundance of context. They’re just hearing the songs and deciding if they like them, unperturbed by the words spilled by critics trying to understand the music. Klosterman himself said he feels like he needs to listen to a record 500 times before he can have an intelligent opinion about it — which is to say, a lot of music criticism would fall under the umbrella of half-baked at best. All of which is a long-winded way of saying: I fall under the spell of opinion and critique too often to the detriment of my own taste. But life is long, if we’re lucky, and middle age is a perfect time to realize that, if you’re going to cut against the grain, at least you’ve spent a respectable amount of time mastering the system up to this point.

Quick hits: Tea originally was a kind of cake that was boiled and drunk. Then it was a powder (matcha?). Not until the 16th century did the steeped version we’re all familiar with become the preferred method of drinking it.
