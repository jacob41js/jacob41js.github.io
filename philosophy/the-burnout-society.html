Personal Reflections

What a strange but insightful treatise we have here. Perhaps this was my first real exposure to Continental philosophy. It took me around a quarter of the work to really get comfortable with Han’s terminology, even though I thought I understood his larger point. Essentially he is making the argument that people living in modern, industrial societies are no longer threatened by physical harms or even psychic distress à la Freud and the late Victorian age. That is because we are no longer living in a disciplinary culture where things are done first and foremost to avoid breaking the rules and being punished for it. We have the fortune to have outgrown that (in a sense paralleling the death of God in Nietzsche’s formulation) and transformed into a culture that no longer says “no” but instead encourages every single person to fulfill themselves for their own benefit. His argument is a little difficult to parse directly — in fact, I don’t know if he gives a standard argument across the entire text except to clarify his terminology with respect to other thinkers who have used the same terminology in basically similar, but slightly incorrect ways. But here are the main points from what I could gather.

First, in more primitive, less automated times, when we were the subjects of a disciplinary culture, the discipline was partly there to enforce a boundary between what was desirable and what was undesirable. This affected our personal lives in the sense that we accepted and adhered to taboos that were passed down through cultural diffusion and religious beliefs over time. Han terms this an immunological framework because in it we are able to clearly define what is us, what is part of us, and what we are trying to keep from us. We are able to say “no” to things, and in fact we do this all the time. This, however, does rob us of some possibilities for human flourishing and basic freedoms.

With the advent of advanced industrial capitalism, especially in the age of automation and digitization, we have transcended that framework to the point that for many people (in my opinion mostly knowledge workers and the upper class, although you certainly see the ramifications on the lower classes) we are no longer constrained because we are trying to differentiate ourselves from the Other, but by the reality of the finitude of time and space in a world where we can say “yes” to literally anything. Because anything is possible, the onus of becoming a productive, fully realized person is no longer outsourced to rituals, traditions, etc., but instead it falls squarely on our shoulders. Every single human has the ability, and therefore must strive, to become — themselves. And this, Han argues, makes us exhausted. He characterizes depression as the burnout we feel as a result of constantly trying to “become who we are” in a world where that could be anything. We no longer feel capable or even morally right in trying to differentiate ourselves from the Other, so we are overwhelmed at all the things that we say yes to.

This phenomenon is accelerated by digital technologies which further the illusion that anything is possible. Our day-to-day lives exist predominantly on digital platforms where person-to-person interaction, commerce, play, work — everything happens seamlessly. And not just seamless in the sense that one blends into the other, but seamless in the sense of being frictionless. The ease of everything fortifies our belief that we can be whatever we want to be, unbound by the constraints of the past. He gives us a rereading of the myth of Prometheus in which the god’s punishment isn’t so much that he has his liver eaten over and over again for eternity, but instead that rather than living in godlike ignorance of this freedom he is condemned to have to figure out every day what the meaning of his life is.

Above I alluded to who I think this work is targeting, and I think it’s pretty clear that the managerial class and up are those predominantly affected by this. I think that people of lower status who really don’t have the luxury of considering these problems may not be living in this new paradigm. However, they could be, I suppose, and the phone addictions, giving children over to screens, overdoses, and all the other societal problems we see could be symptoms or causes of it. The upshot of all this is that we have given up a belief in a higher, transcendent purpose. At the end of the treatise there are a few good paragraphs about how now we are like Nietzsche’s last men who value health simply for the sake of trying to be healthy, not to put ourselves in pursuit of something greater.

I also think there are some resonances with Charles Taylor’s work A Secular Age. Are there any connections between Taylor’s porous vs. buffered selves and Han’s disciplinary vs. burnout societies? That perhaps bears some more pondering, but I think it’s fair enough to say that both men have identified the fact that we no longer live in a world in which ultimate meaning can be taken for granted. It must somehow be crafted intentionally by each and every one of us. Taylor, I think, believes this is more or less a good thing because it does bolster human freedom and allows us to love God fully in the sense that we choose him with open eyes. I’m not sure if Han believes that.

Much of his language is negative sounding, but I don’t get the sense that he’s deliberately advocating a return to more archaic times. And that’s a bit of the problem here. Surely there is value in people being freer. I know that Han is concerned about us using that freedom incorrectly — i.e. auto-violence, allowing capital to use our urge to become who we are to further their profits — but that doesn’t mean he’s against the freedom in and of itself.
