Personal Reflections

This Easter I was bothered by thoughts of death and misfortune. Recently it’s been as if my eyes are wide awake to the undeserved blessings I’ve amassed in 36 years. My marriage that transformed from a high school romance into a durable, indubitable partnership over the course of 21 years is obscenely abnormal. Marlee and I are in love, and we go to the movies together and laugh at the same jokes and cook together and volunteer together at bingo. If I’m not spending the last two hours of my day watching something with her and then talking until we have to sleep, it’s worse than drinking a shot of double espresso before bed.

My daughters are incandescent sprites who seem to make their way through the world with relative ease. They have friends. Their teachers love them. They try new activities and aren’t afraid to talk to people. These are things I struggled with that I don’t seem to have handed on in a seriously detrimental way. The two of them play together like best friends. Anything annoying that comes along with parenting is swamped by the inexplicable goods they are to the world and my life.

Despite never having enjoyed or wanted this career, I’ve stuck around long enough that people respect me, I’m well taken care of, and I have been spared seemingly random layoffs and transitions in the company. I’m not smarter or more hardworking than anyone else as far as I can tell, but a combination of luck, inertia, and competence has landed me in a position I think is hard to compete with.

My parents and in-laws have been beyond generous every step of my life. My house I owe to them. A stable influence in my kids’ life I owe to them. Hair cuts I owe to them. For any petty squabbles we’ve had over the years, none seem worthy of pointing out when stacked against the mountain of evidence that points to unconditional love and support.

So why am I thinking about calamity? Because it seems too good to be true. Sooner or later I’ll be put to the test, or worse, Marlee or M or D will be put to the test. I don’t really know anything about loss, and it seems like it’ll be a bit of a late start in life whenever I first find out. My experience with grief so far has been of the sort where it feels like I’m watching it from behind a window. For the last year when I go to church I think about Sr. Rosie and how we used to play guitar for mass together. Her loss went unfelt by me for years. Now she vaguely haunts my thoughts in expected and unexpected places. Is this grief? I don’t think so. It’s a type of regret for time we didn’t have, questions I didn’t ask, and opportunities I passed up. These are the same feelings I have about my grandparents now, years after they have passed. I can’t empathize with the way my mom felt when both her parents died within a year of each other. What did it feel like to wake up on the first morning of your life when your parents were no longer alive? Much less with my cousins who lost their mom when she was so young — younger than my parents. I hardly even know them, let alone the grief they felt. What does a person like me, lucky beyond my wildest dreams, have to say to anyone who has faced the hardest moments in life and yet persists?

Lewis has a lot to say about the theological implications of grief on the problem of evil, and I take that for what it’s worth. I don’t feel particularly ashamed to say that from this side of the equation I have the feeling that the theology of loss will not be of utmost concern to me whenever something like that happens. Perhaps that makes me a bad Christian and proves that in a real sense my faith doesn’t really matter to me in a substantial way. I don’t have a rebuttal to that. I hope that’s not what such a trial proves. But I also hope that I never have to pray so earnestly for deliverance from something so agonizing. Selfish and short-sighted, I know. But I’ve never found great comfort in facing the unknown. The easiest way to throw me off my game when I played basketball was to take me to a gym in a part of the greater Pittsburgh area I had no knowledge of, against a team I couldn’t envision. The specters of their beings haunted me even as I faced their realities on-court, and I choked in obvious fashion a number of times by this playbook.

So far nothing in this life has made me feel as if I were amputated. Loved ones who have passed have been at enough of a distance that I’ve experienced their loss as more of an uncanniness, and I’m left with strange memories like tchotchkes from vacation — like watching the Fukushima tsunami coverage in my grandparents’ living room after my Pappap died, or being the last to find out my Grandma had passed away. They’re not exactly memories of the person, and I don’t feel loss when I think of them. Instead I have monuments in my memory, the odd-shaped rock that happens to be in the correct place to mark your property corner, that I can follow or not as I please.

I’m old enough now to realize the truth: I am not the main character in this life, things are not destined to work out for me indefinitely. The Mighty Mighty Bosstones probably said it best: “Never had to knock on wood.” Recently on an episode of Shrinking, Harrison Ford’s character said the scars of a 43-year-old Jimmy’s life were the signs of a life fully lived. My lack of scars are my indication that I have a lot more living to do.
