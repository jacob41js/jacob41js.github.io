God in the Machine: Maimonides and Superintelligence

Nick Bostrom successfully scared me into awareness about the threat of artificial intelligence while waiting to get my tires changed at Ambridge Tire Center on Black Friday 2021. His book Superintelligence laid out a convincing and logically coherent case for how the proliferation of AI could eventually lead to the creation of a god-like entity that could easily dispatch humanity in pursuit of its own inscrutable goals, unless, of course, the very humans creating this being solved what was introduced to me as the ‘alignment problem’. The hypothesis rested on the axiom that, definitionally, if artificial intelligences could become self-improving through recursive learning, there is essentially no upper bound on their capabilities save the physical laws that preside over the universe. And who knows, perhaps they would become so advanced that they would solve these physical constraints, too, by their superior ability to reason, tirelessness, and limitless brain capacity.

Very scary stuff. Hence the fearmongering you hear about today regarding AI and the lot of us being turned into paper clips. And yet, while it was and is a frightening proposition, sitting there in the RAV-4 letting the November cold slowly seep into the vehicle, it struck me that this type of argumentation felt vaguely familiar, perhaps a mirror image of something I had encountered before. Applying pure reason to an entity that necessarily exceeds the grasp of the very rationality trying to conceive of it reminded me of the attempt of Medieval European philosophers and theologians to get to heart of who and what God is. My introductory philosophy courses in college (and copy of AFI’s Black Sails in the Sunset album) introduced terms like omnipotence and omniscience, and tried to reason back from those assumed necessary ingredients to what God’s will was, especially with respect to humans who were supposed to have been created in his likeness. Of course there were problems posed that seemed slightly less than serious (how many angles can fit on the head of a pin, anyone?), but at the root of it was a sincere belief that God could be known by virtue of the reason he endowed us with, if only we used it in the right way.

Moses Maimonides, though slightly predating and outside the scholastic movement that really made these arguments famous, in his Guide for the Perplexed, reminded me of this thought as I worked through his dense philosophy of negative theology, Aristotelianism, and cloaked Neoplatonism. Although full recognition didn’t arrive for me until Part III, even in the beginning of this tome Maimonides goes to great lengths trying to explain in common language how it is impossible to speak about God in common terms. Everything is super-this and super-that, whether that be kindness, intelligence, understanding, creativity, cooking, cleaning, parenting — whatever. We add this super in front of everything not to say that God is just that much better than any human, but because it acts more as a crude signifier that we don’t even have the correct word to qualify or quantify the characteristics of God. Everything he is so far exceeds anything that we are that we actually don’t even have words to describe him. Instead we’re left with lame prefixes whose lameness signals our ineptitude and the ineffability of God in our minds.

Great — for all intents and purposes God qua God is unknowable to us. And yet, by virtue of the fact that he created us, he has endowed us with some capacity to understand him and his will in order that we might follow him. (Let’s leave the Active Intellect aside for now, a Neoplatonic flourish that is probably best saved for another day... But if we must, in our analogy, what God is to Artificial Superintelligence [ASI, for short], the Active Intellect is to the executable program that implements the mind of the ASI.) Therefore, our reason can be honed and perfected to the human limit to try to achieve knowledge of God. In sum, that’s what Maimonides is after. TBD on whether he succeeds or not. In my opinion I think he does an impressive job meshing Aristotelian logic and metaphysics with Jewish tradition, but there are definitely some areas where the wheels seem to come off the chariot. Regardless, my point is that there appears to be an analogy between using pure reason to interpret God and to predict the state of a world where ASI exists, despite the fact that we must admit both are ultimately unknowable due to our limitations. So I wonder what, if anything, that can tell us about one or both of those things.

I suppose we’ll start with God, as, by virtue of being the creator of all things, he’s the older of the two. Can we reason ourselves to and through belief in God, and more than that to a particular religious code? Maimonides doesn’t go that far. Revelation is still required to make the tower stand, like it’s the cardboard sleeve of the Jenga set serving as the scaffolding that facilitates the reassembly of the stack of blocks after it all comes tumbling down. However, given his Aristotelian tendencies, he does endorse the idea that belief in a deity is the only rational conclusion about reality, and he shows this through a variety of proofs. Perhaps more importantly, though, he establishes the immateriality and unity of God using only logic, leaving us tethered to the Almighty by a chain of thought that originates in our heads. While I’m sympathetic to his line of argument it does strike me as a bit sterile and possibly myopic. After all, this is rooted in a kind of radical ignorance of what exactly God is, and Maimonides will end up, later in the book, running into issues of reconciling how God has any power over his creation and humans specifically, how free will and Divine Providence can co-exist, and in some cases sounds Wittgensteinian advising us to use caution and not to speak of things that are beyond our comprehension. There are stretches of this treatise where he sounds like a proto-Spinoza positing God as the underlying cause of everything, only a step away from everything being an instance of God. Then there are times where the unknowability of God seems like such a chasm to span, even after achieving the pinnacle of intellect, that you can hear the rapping of Kierkegaard on the door waiting to be let into our nerd-cultish-soiree so we can all take a leap of faith together. Truly The Guide is a maze of reason and faith with no obvious exit but lots of dead ends where philosophers past and present future lurk offering cryptic advice. Every route he takes seems to lead eventually to a clash of reason with the world as we perceive it, whether it’s the problem of evil, predestination, or basic understandings of astronomy and physics. There were even times when I wondered whether Maimonides actually believed the conclusions he was arguing for, or if he was just laying these arguments out as clearly as possible so that the reader could determine for himself what Maimonides could not speak directly — namely, that all of this doesn’t make any sense.

Now, do I think Maimonides really holds that opinion? No, although I think it does illustrate the nature of his doubts, without which, perhaps, you can’t have faith. Given the extensiveness of this tract, plus all the other tomes he composed on Jewish law throughout his life, it doesn’t seem likely to me that he was a secret atheist. I think he really did believe in the God of Moses and was trying very hard to reconcile it with the philosophy he also loved dearly. This is a relevant thread to pull on, though, because what if this maze didn’t start with the Jewish God at one end and Peripatetic thought at the other? I can imagine a world where this is all very path dependent, where you can choose any permutation of divine tradition and philosophical school. Although I haven’t performed the exercise myself, Aquinas would do this for Christianity, and Avicenna would do it for Islam. What this reveals is the case for a specific instance of God is not wedded to a particular system of logic, and therefore as much as any thinker would like to show a demonstrative proof of their preferred God, it’s fungible at the margin. That’s what makes these things arguable in the first place.

More to our purposes, I believe that in modern times this concept is illustrated by the contemporary Rationalist school of thought and the incarnation of artificial superintelligence. My intuition about this analogy is that ASI and rationalist thinking are a particular instance of this duality that seeks to fuse reason and transcendence. One upshot of this is that eventually you reach a point where your intellectual faculties fail and you must default to making assumptions or proposing axioms about something beyond those capabilities to keep the argument going. Your intellect may surpass mine, which may allow you to travel farther down this road (see Maimonides’s explanation of the varying levels of prophets). But there’s no escaping the asymptote; these points will never neatly converge. Now, a corollary is that that our ability to establish demonstrative proofs fails just as we run out of empirical evidence. This is why Maimonides can argue so vociferously against Aristotle with regards to the eternity of the universe or the creatio ex nihilo. As we approach this limit we lose objective benchmarks with which to evaluate the validity of either party’s claims. Finally, Maimonides takes a lot of time and care to establish that God doesn’t share the qualities humans have. Yet, the only way we can come to that conclusion in the first place is to assume that God is something like us, otherwise he would be inconceivable completely. We bring this same baggage to the creation of and debate about AI, especially since we are very aware of the fact that we are the makers responsible for it. It’s not really possible to create something that isn’t in some ways like us (the imagination requires known inputs even if it can create unfamiliar outputs). Acknowledgement of that, though, can only lead us to the realization that however rigorous our thinking, the object of our thought will forever remain infinitely beyond our grasp. And so, just as God necessarily turns out to not be like us, it may also be the case that ASI isn’t either. Further, its ways may be completely opaque and indifferent to us if it achieves its intelligence independent of human intervention (in other words, through a self-directed advancement that takes place because of the substrate humans created but by virtue of its own, proprietary mechanism). That’s not to say that Bostrom doesn’t consider this in his book, because from what I remember he explicitly does. I’m simply noticing that the contours of these debates map onto one another.

I see a lot of online discussion from public intellectuals recently highlighting that with the emergence of AI we are entering a new enchanted age. Reading Maimonides makes me see how that goes beyond people treating AI as an oracle or trying to use it to conjure strange spirits or using it as a way to achieve immortality. In a deeper way, I think it’s going to force us back into these metaphysical debates about the big, unanswerable questions that have been shelved because of the collapse of organized religion in the West. What does transcendence really mean, and can we take part in it? What are human beings for in a universe that is home to an intelligence far beyond our grasp? What makes for a good life if we are no longer the pinnacle of embodied beings? And what if we never were the apex of embodied intelligence, it just took being obviously supplanted for us to realize this? Funnily enough this has some bearing on some other reading I am doing right now. James Bridle’s Ways of Being is testing the waters of other forms of intelligence that we may be overlooking due to our anthropocentric tendencies. ASI may be one of these intelligences, and it may also serve as the mechanism by which we realize that we were never as unique as we imagined.

People commonly reduce religion to humanity’s way of coping with the unknown or the not-yet-understandable. Sure, there’s definitely some of that. But I think what Maimonides and Bostrom show us, despite pondering apparently different things, is that the unknown is not simply something that isn’t solved yet but will be with sufficient time and focus. It’s something innate to the human experience of life that will never be shed. Whether there is a Being that is the author of the universe and knows us individually, or there is an entity we will “create” that will achieve complete knowledge of the universe and thereby us individually, what we’re trying to posit is some “other” that is capable of providing the key to this Gödel-incomplete system that is the human psyche. And what we must acknowledge regardless of the course we chart, is that whatever that existence is will never comport with anything we, with our merely mortal minds, can conceive.
