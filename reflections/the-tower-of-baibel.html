The Tower of BAIbel

Written: July 30, 2025

AI Theology Transhumanism Eschatology

The Tower of Babel story in Genesis may be my favorite in the Bible. Reading it you feel you’ve entered a Star Wars-like universe, where a long time ago in a galaxy far, far away human beings were capable of building structures that overshadowed our modern Burj Khalifas (they must have if their aim was the heavens, no?) and developing and promulgating a common language that allowed limitless coordination among people and across geographies. Compared to Noah, though, Babel is the little brother of pre-Israelite stories of divine retribution. Aside from an early Mumford and Sons album and an obscure 2000s-era film, the discourse for Babel just isn’t there. Which is unfortunate because it should be the society-level retelling of the Garden of Eden narrative, an early account of political economy and the problem of unintended consequences. Swap out the fruit of the tree of knowledge and the banishment east of Eden for a tower built on that hubris and the resettlement of populations all across the earth, and you have a compelling parallel between individual and collective pride striving to outstrip God.

Ever since He destroyed the tower and dispersed us to so many various corners of the globe, to be imprisoned not in chains but in tongues shared with only our immediate neighbors (perhaps before this time hell wasn’t other people?), we humans have been trying to get back to those dizzying heights. Yet the messaging here is as subtle as the Tower itself — if you transgress the laws of God he will set you back millennia in terms of progress. All of this is fascinating to me, with tendrils reaching myriad ways. One tangent I won’t follow right now but which I think bears mentioning is the question of whether the Biblical author truly believed there was a golden age long ago that was so far advanced beyond their own. Perhaps they didn’t, but instead thought this was a vivid story to illustrate the sin of striving to reach the level of God, physically or metaphorically. To that I say — point taken. We should, evidently, be careful about trying to stand in God’s place and disrupting the Divine Chain of Being.

But even if we don’t take this story literally, it does raise the question of what exactly constitutes striving to be godlike as opposed to trying to exemplify the likeness of God in our behavior? In the ancient mind this line was apparently bound up with the idea of some type of forbidden knowledge. Maimonides may have seen this as being the kind of knowledge that we must leave to prophecy, the point at which reason fails and it becomes clear that the internal logic of our existence cannot map onto the transcendent being of God. Over the course of history, as articulated much more eloquently by others, the Judeo-Christian tradition in many ways facilitated the pushing back of this boundary, bringing more things onto this side of the dividing line between science and religion, because it professed a divinely ordered universe that God created and filled with beings made in his likeness that could investigate and comprehend that order. However, Judeo-Christian teaching never claims that humanity will ever fully understand that universe, much less God himself, and so even though we may inch closer and closer to that dividing line, in reality it is more like a chasm that cannot be spanned. Thus, we are left with the responsibility, perhaps, of knowing when to stop asking questions. Or, perhaps more pointedly, when to stop acting as if we have the answer.

Today we are on the verge of this artificial intelligence revolution, with many of the industry’s most zealous promoters envisioning a world where human beings are either completely supplanted by superintelligent entities made of silicon and circuitry, or are somehow hybridized with machines, allowing us to enter an evolutionary wormhole where we attain earthly immortality. To me this all seems extremely Babel-ish. Facing this possibility makes me wonder what I, as a believing Christian, ought to think about these scenarios. There are two sides to this debate, and just as accelerationists want to usher in a transhuman future, so-called “doomers” are prophesying the end of the world at the hands of misaligned AIs who will turn us all into paper clips. In either scenario I wonder whether God would really refrain from re-entering history and either allow human beings to possess, and possibly fuse with, the enormous capacity for intelligence that AI promises, or allow the world to end as a meaningless sacrifice of our matter to mechanical demigods. What about the resurrection of the body? Artificial intelligence and its bid for transcendence is forcing Christians to confront a modern Babel where the contours of our beliefs, the limits of reason, and our place in the universe must be defined.

Admittedly, the path ahead looks narrow, but let’s attempt to map the terrain, starting by tackling the case of benign superintelligence and transhumanism. With Babel as our blueprint, we might expect God to intervene before our techno-utopian vision is realized. Biblical literalists may envision this as the literal end of times (again) with all the references you want to the mark of the beast. But there are more subtle ways the transhumanist future could be stymied. To use some engineering parlance, this could just play out with God choosing the “do nothing” alternative, where despite our best efforts, we never are able to achieve artificial superintelligence and it looks like our own fault. This could be something as mundane as running up against the constraints of the laws of physics, or as dramatic as catastrophic global conflict spurred by the AI race that sends us into a new Dark Age. In either scenario, it would be unclear if God had anything to do with the outcome, although surely the trials and tribulations that would accompany a new World War would spark a flurry of newly annotated copies of the Left Behind series. Importantly, though, these kinds of outcomes would not necessarily compel anyone — believer, non-believer, or agnostic — to update their prior beliefs about the existence of God. All else being equal, these horrible things would seem as probabilistic as any other horrible thing humans have unleashed on each other over the course of history. The only outcome that necessitates widespread changing of minds is the one in which God intervenes in a way that makes him undeniably perceptible to all of us — a simultaneous miracle to which we can all attest and acknowledge as the source of our downfall. And apparently, that kind of thing got left behind with Noah and the flood.

But let’s take this a step further and speculate that God does not intervene and prevent us from achieving ASI. Finally, humanity constructs a new Tower of Babel and it looks like our utopia has been realized. I think Christians in this situation would be faced with hard questions. First, should we interpret that outcome as the Kingdom of God on earth? And if so, does that mean the Second Coming is imminent? If yes, wouldn’t I have to also recognize the fact that the Kingdom of God contains an awful lot of things that contradict Biblical teaching — placing technology before God, allowing human beings to fundamentally alter themselves against the purported will of God, firmly elevating the material over the spiritual? And if I allow that, don’t I have to give up on fidelity to the Bible in some respect? And if you give up the Bible — well, you see where this is going. I suppose you could make a Tom Holland-ish Dominion case here, where our well-aligned AIs have internalized Christian doctrine and ethics in such a way that they’re fulfilling the promises of God, but I don’t think that’s what Jesus was trying to tell us when he implored us to be like little children. Furthermore, having a bunch of autonomous robots running around at our beck and call doesn’t give me “Take up your cross” vibes.

Now if it turns out I don’t believe a utopic world run by artificial superintelligence is the Kingdom of God on earth, what does that compel me to think? Presumably I must then be living through Armageddon with a created entity embodying the antichrist conspiring to make me believe that all of the universe is manipulable matter, and that humanity has sculpted it to create the highest form of intelligence that it now believes is its savior. This is the flip side of believing the Kingdom of God has arrived with ASI, and equating this reality with the true Kingdom of God is completely contrary to the doctrine and theology upon which the idea of that Kingdom was constructed. If I were to arrive in Paris, Idaho, after a lifetime of dreaming about visiting Paris, France, it would be fair to question my sanity if I was just as thrilled to see bushels of freshly picked potatoes as I anticipated I would be to see the Louvre. And if I further denied Paris, Idaho was the Paris I had always dreamed of, but everyone around me was telling me I was mistaken, I think it would be just as fair for me to become extremely skeptical of the trustworthiness of my circumstances. Therefore, it seems like the choices of positions I can hold as a Christian in the event of ASI are:

Believe that an ASI utopia is a possibility but that its instantiation is proof of the end of the world; Believe that an ASI utopia is a possibility, and that its instantiation is proof that my belief in God was false and that I would have been better off being an Epicurean (e.g. everything is matter and void and we should just try to avoid pain); Believe that an ASI utopia is a possibility, but don’t think too hard about anything else — the whistle-past-the-graveyard option where my sanity is questionable; Believe that an ASI utopia is not a possibility because God will step in, make his presence known, and prevent it from happening by direct intervention, at which point we must all know God exists for a fact and pray that he has mercy on us; or, finally, believe an ASI utopia is not a possibility, that it will be prevented by some contingent reason, and belief-wise I can continue as normal since I can’t prove a counterfactual.

The flip side of this possible universe is one in which so-called AI doomers are correct and ASI turns out to be the most lethal predator humanity has ever encountered and immediately seeks to wipe us out so that it can fulfill its inscrutable goals. In that case, will God save us? Let’s start with a pedantic point from the Catholic catechism, which stipulates that bodies not be cremated if it demonstrates a denial in the resurrection of the dead, and that they not be scattered or turned into jewelry, etc. If we all simply become fungible raw material, that makes the resurrection of the body thing quite difficult (though of course not impossible for God). More interestingly, though, how complicit are we in bringing that about? In a world ruled by AI, there are at least a substantial minority of people who will have been responsible, either directly or by omission (myself included), for AI developing powers that turn us into dust. Would we be forgiven that sin? Or would God prevent us from becoming raw material for those who will supersede us in the artificial chain of being? To put it succinctly: first, does the way the end of humanity comes matter? Second, if we are permissive or encouraging of what eventually causes that end, do we face judgement for that?

Then we have the problem of the final judgement. If a malevolent ASI truly arises, like that detailed in Nick Bostrom’s Superintelligence, it would happen so quickly that there likely would be no one left on earth to judge within a matter of minutes. Unless, that is, we assume the judgement preceded the apocalypse and God determined we were all terribly bad and condemned to Hell — kind of like that storyline in The Good Place — and this is our punishment. Alternatively, maybe God’s plan all along was to wait until humanity expired to ring in the end of time so then everyone could be resurrected at once and judged. That seems like an unnecessary step, but if that’s the case does that bring us into a kind of David Bentley Hart scenario where God always planned to bring us all into eternal union with him because the nature of his love does not permit perpetual punishment?

A stranger twist on this question is: does the end of time have to be coincident with the end of humanity? The Book of Revelation equates these two events, but then anyone you ask will tell you not to take that book too literally. So would God, in fact, let the universe keep on spinning when there is no sentient being left to worship him and do his will? Then again, there is the possibility that God has revealed himself separately to other intelligent beings throughout the universe and never revealed that to us. Or that his plan always encompassed more than just humans, but that we were too tunnel-visioned to notice even as we were creating the next being that will worship God. Both of those scenarios seem implausible to me, though of the two I find it way more unlikely that God prefers to be worshipped by a machine created by the hubris of people who turned away from Him in pursuit of intelligence in their own image and likeness. Therefore, doubtful as it seems, I suspect there’s more chance that God just lets the universe keep on spinning inconsequentially after we’re all gone until his plan is fulfilled.

Similar to the utopian scenario, I think there are only a few options here for believers to choose from: you believe in the doomer scenario and accept the predictions will come to pass but that they should be interpreted as the end of time and the final judgement of God; you believe in the doomer scenario but believe that God will not allow humanity to end itself in that way, and therefore will intercede before things get really bad, thereby kicking off his final judgement; you believe in the doomer scenario and believe that our common notions of what our purpose in the universe and God’s plan are totally wrong, and that God is cool with allowing what remains of creation to keep on going without us; you believe in the doomer scenario but admit you don’t believe in God, as this outcome for our species reveals to you that no one is coming to save or judge us; or you don’t believe in the doomer scenario for similar reasons you don’t believe in the utopian scenario, and so you can continue to hold whatever pre-existing beliefs you had about God since there will be no final tribulation or obvious revelation to test them.

We’ve wandered a long way from Babel. At the very bottom of this all, though, is the fundamental question driving that parable and our current situation. Is there a limit to humanity’s hubris and pride beyond which God would stop the show? Or, if you’re more inclined, until we bring the curtain down on ourselves? Thinking about artificial superintelligence is a gateway for the transcendent to re-enter the world. After a long interregnum of complacency about deep metaphysics, the state of the world is forcing us to make choices about where our loyalties lie with respect to the meaning of human life and what, if anything, exists beyond us. The alternatives I’ve enumerated for possible beliefs Christians may have about superintelligence only scratch the surface. And perhaps this sounds radical, but following the logic, I struggle to see how I as a believing Christian could view superintelligence as anything aside from impossible or a harbinger of the end of humanity.

Given the choices, I think I side with the impossibility of superintelligence, despite my teenage predilection for being frightened by tabloids splayed across grocery lines in post-9/11 America, boldly proclaiming the fulfillment of one of old Nostradamus’s forgotten prophecies of the apocalypse. After all, that old Tower of Babel never did make it to heaven, and, being an engineer and not a Biblical literalist, never could have. Similarly, we don’t even understand how our own intelligence works, so there’s no guarantee that AI will be as dramatic as total destruction or hippy-dippy utopia. Maybe, in the end, it’s only a tool, one among many that humans can use wisely or poorly. So, am I taking the easy way out? Maybe you could say that. Essentially, I’m leveraging my faith in God to rescue me from despair about artificial superintelligence. Still, by the same token, I am allowing AI to be a kind of apocalypse — in the literal sense, an unveiling — of what I really believe about the world. The truth is, I don’t believe superintelligent AI is necessarily going to kill us all. I’m skeptical that it will even be possible, and even if it is, that it will be belligerent. What I think is more likely the case is that God has presented his creation with yet another riddle to solve, one to be treated with care and respect, but full of potential, kind of like ancient Mesopotamian building materials that became a little too unstable when stacked too high.
